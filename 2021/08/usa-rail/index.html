How to Cross the Continent by Train, Twice

Dos and don’ts for a near-circumnavigation of the United States.

August 25, 2021

Suppose—hypothetically—that you find yourself one evening, early in the second summer of our pandemic, reading your email, when you open up an advertisement for discounted train tickets. Suppose that you have recently looked into cross-country airline tickets and found yourself dismayed at the prices, compared to what you’d grown accustomed to over the past year. And suppose that your work over that same period has largely consisted of talking to small boxes on your computer screen, and that it is finally summertime, and that many countries are not allowing you to travel to visit them, and that you know that New Mexico is a real place that is supposed to be nice and which lies somewhere between you, sitting at your computer in the Northeast, and the other side of the country. Only then might you—a person who would never be swayed by something as trivial as an advertisement—begin to consider the possibility of your getting on a train, sitting on it for two weeks, and in doing so crossing almost the entirety of the country.

There are several considerations that might make the proposition more attractive. First, the price—ten trips on nearly any Amtrak train, at any time in a thirty-day window, all for the cost of a one-way plane ticket from Boston to Seattle. Seeing as you can cross the country in as little as two trips, this leaves significant flexibility to spend a day or two at points along your route, or to travel on more scenic segments. You may pull up the Amtrak System Map, tracing possible paths across the country, through towns you’ve never heard of—Winnemucca, Needles, Kannapolis. You may be swept up in the majesty of Amtrak’s promotional photographs, imagining yourself on one of the silver trains curving through ochre canyons at sunrise, enjoying your breakfast in the dining car.

Just as you convince yourself that the whole thing is a good idea, and you start to pore over timetables in order to string together ten trip segments into a continental journey, several doubts will begin to nag you. (Delays! The sheer distance! The time! Your work! Food! Delays! Coronavirus! Showering?) But you will push these aside as you put in your credit card information and purchase the ten-trip pass.

Now that you are committed, several things will happen which may have changed your mind but which you cannot control and which it is too late to do anything about. This is good, because it will prepare you for being on the train, where you cannot control anything and the time to change your mind was eight hundred miles ago at the last stop with an airport.

First, you will learn, by calling Amtrak about another matter entirely, that on account of this being the hottest summer ever recorded on Earth, a large fire has severely burned a wooden rail trestle north of Sacramento. Rather than attempting to rush across the weakened trestle at speed, à la Phileas Fogg at Medicine Bow, Amtrak is pleased to inform you that you will be put on a bus instead. Annoyance quickly turns to relief as you learn after this call that the only possible rail detour involves going through Donner Pass. You may not know much about Donner Pass, but you do remember that it is not named after people who were particularly successful in crossing it.

The friendly customer service representative will also inform you that because of the coronavirus, only sleeping-car passengers will be able to eat real food in the dining car. You will be consigned to the café car. This at first may not sound so bad to you; after all, you like cafés. Do not be deceived.

You will awake several days later to an email from Amtrak about a bus. Not the bus that will spare you from crossing Donner Pass, but another bus, running from Boston to Albany and replacing the very first leg of your trip. What the email will not tell you is that the bus will be well air-conditioned and will have a bus conductor, who will be exceptionally cheery and will hand out free snacks at regular intervals. Moreover, the bus will arrive early into Albany, allowing you time to explore this gem of a city on the Hudson.

A few hours later, impressed by the ornate state capitol building and vaguely disturbed by the Brutalist plaza it overlooks, you will with some relief board the train, the Lake Shore Limited, and proceed uneventfully to Buffalo, arriving shortly after midnight to a late-summer thunderstorm.

In the morning, you might wander around a Buffalo neighborhood, or visit the historic, Olmsted-designed Delaware Park. On the way, you might pass an abandoned state hospital, which will make you start to wonder why so many public buildings in upstate New York look like castles. You might hope to find an explanation later, but any such thoughts will soon be pushed from your mind, because you will arrive at Niagara Falls.

Being the sort of person who will take a two-week rail trip to avoid paying higher airfares, you of course will not arrive in Niagara in style. Rather, you will probably haul yourself and all your luggage across town, where you will catch a city bus that will deposit you an hour later in front of a Fairfield Inn a few blocks from the visitor center. But your fare of only $2 will make every minute worth it.

Having grown up nowhere nearby, you will struggle to comprehend that four of the five Great Lakes are draining through this one point, pouring over this cliff at an absolutely terrific rate (enough to fill your neighborhood swimming pool in a tenth of a second). But you must try to move past this and make sure to get yourself to the Cave of the Winds, though not a cave, will still be possibly the best thing you have done in a very long time.

A long, damp elevator ride will take you to a longer, damper tunnel, at the end of which you will find a set of wooden platforms absolutely soaking in the runoff of Bridal Veil Falls, which is now directly above you. The oversized yellow ponchos and cheesy flip flops you wear will much improve the experience, and you will find yourself uncontrollably laughing with complete strangers as you surrender yourselves to the columns of spray bombarding you from 181 feet above.

Reboarding the Lake Shore Limited at midnight, after watching the sun set over Lake Erie, you will be faced with the first challenge of your trip: sleeping on the train. If you can sleep on airplanes, you will be pleasantly surprised. If you cannot—well—you will be feeling less pleasant, especially if you make the mistake of allowing yourself to be stuck in the aisle seat with nowhere to rest your head.

The sunrise will wake you just as the train rolls into South Bend, which will remind you of Pete Buttigieg and his upward failure to the top of the Department of Transportation, which you suppose probably has something to do with Amtrak. This will not improve your mood, and you will be glad to finally reach Chicago.

Stepping off the train, you will smell pretzels, though you will not be sure whether this is because you are in Chicago, or because you have had little sleep and no breakfast and some part of your wants you to eat a giant pretzel. The folks who set Amtrak’s timetables, who you will appreciate more and more as your journey continues, have given you around five hours before your next train leaves. This is ample time to circle the Loop. And to do something about that pretzel.
